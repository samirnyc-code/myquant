In this lesson, you’ll explore the critical role of the US dollar in the global economy and learn about the milkshake theory from its founder, Brent Johnson of Santiago Capital. This session brings together macro trading experts to discuss why understanding dollar dynamics is essential for navigating today’s volatile markets and identifying trading opportunities across asset classes.

Brent explains that the dollar underlies the entire global economy and acts as the environment in which all global trading operates. He emphasizes that almost every major crisis or drawdown over the past 50 years happened when one currency moved dramatically versus another, making it nearly impossible to understand global markets without tracking dollar movements. The dollar’s dominance stems from factors including the depth and liquidity of the US bond market, which is much larger than the equity market.

The milkshake theory emerged from Brent’s analysis that global debts had reached a size that would cause problems worldwide and push interest rates higher rather than lower. He predicted this would trigger a currency crisis and cause the dollar to strengthen as global capital flows to the United States. While a full currency crisis hasn’t materialized, the dollar did strengthen significantly, and asset classes moved largely as predicted through periods of high volatility. Over the last five to six years, there’s been no question that global capital has flowed to the US, though this pattern has shifted somewhat recently.

Jay Mitro, ex-Lehman Brothers trader, reinforces these observations by referencing the TIC long-term purchase data showing $259.4 billion in foreign money flowing into US assets—the second highest level since 2000. This data, collected by the Department of the Treasury, confirms the capital flows Brent describes. Jay also discusses how the traditional understanding of central bank balance sheets and credit creation has fundamentally changed, with the Bank of England in 2014 and the Bank of International Settlements adopting new models that reverse the relationship between reserves and credit creation taught in traditional business schools.

Despite headlines about alternative systems like BRICS nations creating their own currency arrangements for oil trading in Chinese-denominated currency, the actual data doesn’t yet show significant change in the dollar’s dominance. The dollar’s advantages include the unmatched depth and liquidity of US markets, particularly the bond market, which remains the foundation of global finance.
