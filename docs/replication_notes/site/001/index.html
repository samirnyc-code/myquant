I checked the 1D Expected Move against 36 years

What I did

The band

# textbook 1-day expected move move = spot × (VIX / 100) × √(1/365) d1_max = spot + move d1_min = spot − move

That’s the standard “VIX implies a daily move of VIX/√365” convention — no fitted parameters, nothing tuned. Yesterday’s SPX close and yesterday’s VIX close, both known before the session opens. Everything is close-to-close on SPX, no lookahead.

Data: SPX daily OHLC and VIX daily close, both from Yahoo Finance (^GSPC and ^VIX), 1990-01-02 to 2026-07-17. Free and reproducible by anyone. VIX only starts in 1990, which is the only reason the test doesn’t go back further.

To be clear about what this is and isn’t: I have no idea how MenthorQ actually calculates their levels, and I’m not claiming this is their method. I just built a plain vanilla expected-move band and checked whether the published hit-rates were in the right ballpark over a longer history. They were.

The bit I found most interesting

The band gets less reliable as VIX rises

The hit-rate isn’t constant. A vol-scaled band is well behaved in calm markets and gets noticeably leakier when volatility is already elevated — which is exactly when you’re most likely to be leaning on it.

Why I think this is worth knowing

The gap between 74% and 57%

Across 36 years the next close lands inside the band about 74% of the time — but the band is never touched at all on only 57% of sessions. That 17-point gap is sessions that traded through a boundary and recovered.

If you sell premium at these boundaries — condors, strangles, 0DTE spreads — that gap seems like the whole ballgame. Held to settlement, the number that matters is 74%. But if you have a stop, a delta band, or just a limited appetite for sitting underwater, you get tested a lot more often than the headline suggests. And in a VIX 30+ tape, “never touched” drops to 43% — you should probably expect to be tested most days.

Which is really just an independent confirmation of what MenthorQ already tells people in that first quote: actively manage them, don’t let them sit.

A high win rate is not an edge

Worth stating plainly, because a 74% number invites exactly the wrong conclusion. I backtested a naive 0DTE iron condor with the shorts parked at the band edges. It won 77% of the time and still lost money (profit factor 0.85) — the losers were bigger than the win rate could pay for. A band that contains price 74% of the time is a description of volatility, not a strategy.

Caveats

What this is and isn’t

  • A roughly 1σ vol band containing price ~74% of the time is close to what theory predicts anyway. The interesting part to me was the stability across 36 years, not the level itself.
  • I don’t know MenthorQ’s actual methodology and I’m not guessing at it. This is just a vanilla band compared against their public numbers.
  • SPX only, close-to-close, daily bars. Intraday path is not modelled beyond the touched/not-touched split.
  • Yahoo Finance is a free, unofficial source. Index history does get revised occasionally.
  • None of this is financial advice. I did it for fun on a weekend because I wanted to know if the numbers held up. Do your own work before risking money on any of it.

Independent analysis, shared in good faith. I’d genuinely welcome corrections — if I’ve made an error in the method or the data, please tell me.
