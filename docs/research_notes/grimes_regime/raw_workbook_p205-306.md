# Raw extraction — Grimes Course Workbook, PDF pages 205–306 (pullbacks, Anti, S/R, MA-cross studies, DJIA decade stats)
Extracted 2026-07-22 (S81). PDF page cites. Pages 250–296 mostly DJIA history charts.

## Bias validation / regime-contradiction logic (pp. 205–207)
- p.206: A directional bias (regime call) is only meaningful if you can state what would disprove it. Quantifiable contradiction signals for a BEARISH bias: (1) "we should not see many consolidation patterns resolve easily to the upside", (2) "we should not see too many large standard deviation spikes to the upside." Either persisting = evidence contradicting the call. → Implementable regime-flip triggers: count consolidation-breakout directions + large-sigma bars by direction.
- p.206: Regime lives on the higher timeframe (daytrader bias from weekly/daily); trading TF trends both ways within HTF bias.
- p.207: A good statistical bias that fails "may fail dramatically" — failed regime structures produce tradeable opposite moves (transitions are high-energy).

## Pullback trading = trend-regime logic (pp. 213–217)
- p.213: Preconditions: market must be BOTH trending AND fluctuating. Very strong trends that don't pull back = distinct runaway sub-regime → stand aside for pullback systems.
- p.214: Band calibration rule (key quant spec): bands "should contain roughly 80%–90% of the price action." Wide middle range of MA length/width all work — pick settings and never change them.
- p.214-215: Mechanic: strong move confirmed by band touch → enter around a middle (near the MA). The MA has no magic — edge is from the strong-move-then-mean-return structure, works across many MA lengths.
- p.215: Exhaustion filter: avoid entries after climactic moves — "large range with trend bars that often extend far beyond the [band] edge." Distinguishing with-trend strength from exhaustion = "perhaps the key technical skill of with-trend trading."
- p.216: Entry: LTF breakout (previous bar's high) or LTF pivots/failure tests. Stop: beyond previous swing extreme; guideline 2–3 ATR beyond entry. First partial at 1R. Pyramiding: sequential pullbacks, total open risk never exceeds one trade's risk (p.216-217).

## The Anti = transition-regime pattern (pp. 218–219)
- p.218: "the first pullback after a potential trend change... a pullback that must be set up by strong momentum" — momentum AGAINST the prior trend. Classic pullback = trend regime; Anti = transition regime. Rarer subset.
- p.218-219: Quantification checklist: (1) what defines the prior established trend; (2) what degree of countertrend momentum triggers; (3) entry; (4) stop/target. Engine mapping: established trend + strong opposing momentum burst = "transition" state; first pullback of the new direction = first confirmation of new regime.

## S/R behavior taxonomy (pp. 219–222)
- p.219: Four outcomes at a level: holds with immediate rejection / holds without clear rejection / fails with action around the level / fails with no action. Plus slow rejection (penetration → consolidation on the level → strong reversal).
- p.220: Levels are zones; bar range/volatility often changes at working levels.
- p.221-222: Random lines look equally significant; floor-trader pivots "may be no better than your random levels." Fibonacci: no quantitative support (pp.207-212). MAs do NOT act as S/R (p.215, 300).

## Core statistical worldview (pp. 223–224)
- p.223: "Most of the time, markets are very close to efficient... price movement is random and we have no reason, from a technical perspective, to be involved." Default regime = NO-TRADE. Edges from repeatable patterns are "very small."

## Methodology traps (pp. 225–230)
- Day-of-month/week fallacy = future leakage; no first-day-of-month effect once window properly defined. Universal check: "Always ask how could you trade the given effect." "When we find something that appears to be a statistical homerun, we've probably made a mistake somewhere" (p.225). A trader lost high seven figures on a leaked statistic (p.226).

## Death/Golden Cross event studies (pp. 231–239)
- Death Cross (50d under 200d), Russell 2000 cash, 19 events: −1.92% one week out vs +0.2% baseline → statistically significant VERY short-term sell signal, "strong for a week, and then decays"; no longer-term significance.
- Golden Cross on R2K: NO significant effect at any horizon. DJIA back to 1920s: no effect for either cross. 100 stocks × 10 yrs: no clear effect; slight negative excess is a selection artifact (MA-cross events select mid-range days).
- p.239: Baselines: stocks up/unch/down = 50.6/1.02/48.38% daily. "Individual stocks mean revert strongly" — mean reversion after down closes "almost overwhelming over some timeframes"; why quantitative trend-following struggles on stocks. p.302: "one of the strongest statistical tendencies in the market is for stocks to mean revert after large one day moves."

## Nested pullback (pp. 240–242)
- HTF flag/consolidation in force toward a target; a 2–3 bar pause on the trading TF within that move = entry. Resolution character (clean breakdown vs failure) reveals conviction. Hard to quantify: needs definitions for initial move, momentum threshold, pause scope.

## Failure test (pp. 243–244)
- Wyckoff spring/upthrust, Sperandeo 2B: attempt beyond S/R fails — "often an aggressive countertrend pattern... often mark at least short-term ends of trends, and sometimes major turning points." Especially informative around the current session high/low intraday. Resolves fast — profits or stops within a few bars.

## DJIA decade regime statistics (pp. 253–277)
- Bull decades: 1920s +128.5% (324 new-52wk-high days vs 45 low days), 1950s +241.6% (328/13), 1990s +309.1% (315/5), 2010-2017 +111.7% (261/3). → 52-week new-high/new-low day-count ratio = clean slow regime marker.
- Bear/flat: 1930s −38.5% (157/104; avg 20d Hvol 29.3% — highest; peak 243.5% 3/15/1933), 1970s +3.7% (94 highs vs 109 lows — only decade with more lows), 2000s −8.2% (128/47; Hvol 20.9%).
- Vol regime marker: avg 20-day Hvol 10.4% (1950s/60s quiet bull) to 29.3% (1930s). High-vol decades = bear/crisis.
- Downside sigma-day counts exceed upside in almost every decade (1940s: 134 days <−2σ vs 91 >+2σ) — persistent negative skew of daily extremes.
- p.279: for shocks, ask "In how many of these cases was the market already trending in the direction of the eventual shock?"

## Foundational testability (pp. 297–305)
- p.300: "you can't have a relationship in the data that is both real and important, and also invisible." An event after which the market behaves no differently than baseline "was, by definition, a non-event." Bollinger "±2 SD contains 95%" claim bogus (returns not normal).
- p.304-305: "Thinking conceptually": strong impulse + shallow/quiet consolidation without backing off much = "a market consolidating to go higher" — continuation regime; the exact shape/ratio of the consolidation doesn't matter. Not every tick matters — many prints are noise.

## Explicit NO-TRADE conditions collected
- Market efficient/random most of the time → default flat (p.223).
- Trending but NOT fluctuating (runaway trend) → no pullback entries (p.213).
- Post-climactic bar (large range, far beyond band edge) → no with-trend entry (p.215).
- No definable contradiction condition for your bias → bias invalid (p.206).

## What does NOT work (avoid as regime inputs)
Fibonacci (pp.207-212, 300-301); MAs as S/R (p.215, 300); Golden Cross (no effect); Death Cross (1-week R2K only); calculated pivots possibly random (p.222); day-of-week/month seasonality (leakage); Hindenburg Omen "meaningless" (p.231).

## Top implementable takeaways
(1) 80–90% price-containment band calibration; (2) directional counts of consolidation resolutions + large-sigma bars as regime-contradiction evidence; (3) impulse + quiet shallow consolidation = continuation, large-range beyond-band bars = exhaustion/transition; (4) Anti structure as formal transition state; (5) failure test at extremes as turning-point marker; (6) 52-week (or session) new-high/new-low ratio + realized-vol level as slow regime markers; (7) mean-reversion dominance after large one-day moves (stocks/index); (8) "could you have traded it on the timeline" leakage check.
