What Adam Grimes gives us for a market regime engine

Full read of The Art & Science of Technical Analysis (Wiley 2012, 480 pp) and the Art & Science of Trading course workbook (609 pp), distilled to what is buildable for an ES regime classifier: bull / bear / transition / no-trade.

Prepared for: Thomas Date: 2026-07-22 Sources cited by page in repo: docs/research_notes/grimes_regime/

01The model in one paragraph

Markets are near-efficient most of the time; most price movement is random, so the engine's default state is no-trade. All action lives between two poles — range expansion (momentum, positive autocorrelation) and mean reversion (negative autocorrelation). Aggregated, the two cancel to roughly zero autocorrelation, which is why unconditioned studies find nothing: the engine's whole job is conditioning first, then measuring. The cycle is Wyckoff's (accumulation → markup → distribution → markdown), and the interfaces between trend and range are where both the opportunity and the failure modes live.

"Markets tend to work in mean reversion mode after a period of expanded volatility, and in range expansion mode after a period of contracted volatility." workbook p.594–602

Volatility clustering is the one robust nonrandom feature he stands behind: shocks persist (GARCH-like), magnitude is predictable, direction is not. His summary of trading failure: "Most trading losses come from incorrectly identifying the emerging volatility environment."

02The four states, in Grimes terms

Bull — trend up

  • Structure first: higher pivot highs and higher lows on ATR-filtered zigzag swings; up-legs longer than down-legs in price and time.
  • Pullbacks retracing 40–80% of the prior leg are normal (futures: mean 62%, SD 21%) — deeper retracements actually precede larger extensions.
  • Confirmation: price one side of the 20-EMA; MACD (SMA 3-10-16) new momentum highs; failed divergences piling up is itself trend confirmation.

Bear — trend down

  • Mirror structure: LL + LH required together.
  • Not symmetric with bull: declines carry expanding volatility; bear rallies are sharp and vicious vs orderly bull pullbacks.
  • In his futures tests the trend-state edge is strongest on the short side (triple-MA down state: −203 bp excess).

Transition

  • Hard 2-step rule: (1) a break event — climax bar ≥3× average range at a new extreme, free bars outside the Keltner channel, three pushes, failure test, valid divergence, or first counter-swing bigger than the prior with-trend swing — then (2) a new momentum extreme in the opposite direction.
  • Divergence alone never flips state — "divergences fail about as often as they work."
  • Most terminations resolve to range, not reversal — transition defaults back to no-trade.

No-trade — default

  • Price chopping both sides of the 20-EMA: absence of departure "virtually guarantees that an imbalance does not exist."
  • Range interiors are a random walk — only the edges (springs / upthrusts / failure tests) are tradable.
  • Sub-states: both-band spike (triangle forming), expanding range, post-climax cooldown, and the runaway "slide-along-band" trend — directional but unenterable.

Asymmetries to encode: entry to trend is easy to miss but cheap to wait for (most breakouts fail — demand higher-lows-into-level, tight sub-range <25% at the edge, volatility expansion at the break). Exit from trend needs the full 2-step rule. Transition resolves to no-trade unless the opposite Dow structure completes.

04The volatility layer

A second state machine runs in parallel and decides which force dominates:

  • Compressed (5-day / 40-day ATR ratio < 0.5, or realized vol in its bottom 20th percentile): expect range expansion. His vol-compression breakout test is the strongest futures continuation signal in either book — trigger day's true range ≥ 5-day ATR with a strong close: +128 bp** by day 4, 73% up at day 5. Suppress mean-reversion plays here.
  • Expanded (σ-spike ≥ 2.5–3.0, where spike = today's return ÷ yesterday's 20-day return stdev): expect mean reversion — with the futures caveat below. Shocks persist; don't expect a quick return to quiet.

07Tested dead ends — do not build on these

  • Fibonacci retracements/extensions — swing ratios indistinguishable from a random walk (median unfiltered retracement ≈ 100%).
  • Moving averages as support/resistance; golden cross (no effect); death cross (one-week R2K effect only); MA touch-and-hold.
  • Floor-trader pivots (≈ random lines); day-of-week/month seasonality (leakage artifact); opening skew (arcsine-law artifact).
  • Volume confirmation around springs/upthrusts — he could not substantiate it.
  • Fading big single days in futures; fading breakouts during volatility compression.

08Validation protocol (his "Pythia" discipline)

  • Assign each bar's return to the previous bar's state (no look-ahead); compare per-state excess return / stdev / %Up vs unconditional ES baseline at bars +1…+20.
  • Run every feature against random-walk, AR(2), and GARCH synthetic nulls — anything that lights up on those is noise.
  • ≤ 3 conditions per rule; single-use out-of-sample; discard effects < 10 bp or with mean/median sign disagreement.
  • Regime labels must be assignable in real time (a priori) — post-hoc trend/range labeling is circular.
  • Test across volatility regimes (our tick data spans 2021-06 → 2026-07: low-vol 2021, 2022 bear, 2024-26).
