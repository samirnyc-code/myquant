## Breakout quality / regime-transition signatures (Ch.6 end)
- p.193-194 — Prebreakout base: springs (drops below base support that immediately recover) = accumulation evidence. Higher lows into resistance = prebreakout accumulation.
- p.195 — Base failure criterion: quick drop below base support with rapid recovery is normal; but consolidation BELOW the broken support level shifts probabilities to breakdown.
- p.196 — Turtle-style 20/55-day channel breakouts: most such levels are NOT "real, visible" levels; Turtle success was systematic capture of every trend, not the level itself.
- p.197 — First pullback after breakout = confirmation the new trend regime is live. Requires good activity (volume, volatility, price action) beyond the level. Breakout level itself is a BAD stop/regime reference — strongest continuations dip back below the level to trap shorts. Valid reference = prebreakout pivot low: a drop below it indicates failed breakout.
- p.198 — After one successful confirming pullback, a breakout "is simply a trend trade" — regime formally transitions to trend. Trends from good breakout levels tend to be "exceptional trends... extend for several legs."
- p.199-201 — "Most breakouts fail" (explicit). Two failure diagnostics: (1) weak momentum/volume/interest beyond the level → likely spring/upthrust; (2) character of first reaction — a very strong first counter-swing after the breakout = greatly increased failure probability. Breakout failure always manifests as failure of the first pullback.
- p.203-204 — Full regime cycle map (Wyckoff overlay): springs/failure tests at range bottom → base → breakout (accumulation→markup transition) → first pullback → pullbacks as trend gains steam → complex pullbacks as trend matures ("monitoring the strength of pullbacks gives insight into trend integrity") → failure test at eventual high → Anti at the high → upthrusts at prior extreme → breakdown. Trades Grimes explicitly rejects (no edge over large sample): trades inside ranges, simple buying/selling at support, fading overextended markets. "Common for trends to end with a final failure test at the highs"; markup→distribution transitions should be avoided (chop); the Anti is the designated tool for turns.

## Moving averages as regime filters (Ch.7)
- p.206-207 — No statistical edge exists AT any moving average. Valid uses are regime-confirmation only: (1) price spending most time on one side of an MA = trend that direction; (2) MA slope: flat = trading range, sloped = trend; same spot can be uptrend/downtrend/range on different MA lengths (all simultaneously valid, different time frames).
- p.208-209 — NO-TRADE rule (key): equilibrium = random walk = no edge. Detection: price chopping back and forth on both sides of an intermediate-term moving average. Significant departure from the MA is necessary-but-not-sufficient evidence of imbalance; absence of departure "virtually guarantees that an imbalance does not exist." Grimes's core stand-aside filter.
- p.209-211 — Don't initiate far from the MA (overextension); MA is a pullback reference in NORMAL trends; very strong trends may not reach it. Mechanical slope-filtering discouraged (lag); price structure leads MA slope changes.

## Keltner channels — quantified extension gauge
- p.212 — Bollinger critique: real containment ~88% of closes within 2 SD of 20-SMA (not 96%); "Bollinger balloon" after big bars.
- p.212, Table 7.1 p.214 — Spec: modified Keltner channels at 2.25 ATR around a moving average, calibrated to contain 85–90% of trading range. Empirical, 2,403,774 bars: Inside% mean — large-caps 87.7%, small-caps 85.4%, futures 85.9% (min 77.8/max 92.5 across 16 contracts), forex 89.8%, random data 86.8%. "Free bars" (entire bar outside channel): stocks 3.4%/4.3%, futures 3.8%, forex 2.3%, random 3.8%.
- p.214 — Slight statistical edge to fading moves to the bands (claimed, unquantified). Fade only when extended beyond bands; fading equilibrium markets = steady losses.
- p.215 — With-trend regime entry: after a close outside the band, enter pullback near mid-channel/MA in that direction — "reliable trade."
- p.216-217 — Regime signature "slide outside the bands": market presses into a band and STAYS = one-sided extreme imbalance; no pullbacks; often a HTF climax with severe snapback risk. Explicit stand-aside: "The best course of action is almost always to have no position." Normal strength → buyable pullback; extreme strength usually indicates exhaustion ("a very strong move often means the party is over").
- p.217-218 — Regime signature "spike through both bands": sharp move through one band immediately reversed through the other → expect triangle-type consolidation around a central price → "difficult trading environment that is best avoided."

## MACD as momentum/regime tool (Ch.7)
- p.219 — "Momentum precedes price": significant new high/low on fast line → next pullback likely continuation. Quantification hint: express indicator as percentile of a look-back window; enter retracements after excursion into top/bottom decile; 40-bar history as starting look-back.
- p.221 — Climax detection: momentum move "out of all proportion to the recent history" → do NOT treat pullback as continuation. When MACD rescales so recent history compresses to a tight wiggle, disregard the indicator until conditions normalize (also after big overnight gaps intraday).
- p.222-223 — Divergence: "divergences fail about as often as they work"; "any extended trend will set up multiple momentum divergences, most of which will fail." Working definition of trend: "trends will also roll over momentum divergences" (repeated failed divergences = confirmed trend regime).
- p.224-225 — Divergence validity requires an actual retracement swing between the two compared points; a market simply sliding higher gives false divergences. Divergence reset/expiry (quantified): fulfilled/reset when (1) price returns to an intermediate-term (~20-period) MA, (2) fast line crosses zero from the divergent state, or (3) 10+ bars elapse. Consider computing off typical price or midpoint.
- p.225 — Avoid buying with fast line extended into highs / selling into lows (overextension filter).
- p.226-227 — Fast line "drive and hold" (pegged in overbought) = real strength, strong trend regime — NOT a fade signal. Only look for divergence AFTER the fast line touches/nears zero.
- p.227 — Fast-line hook systematic rule example: enter short on first bar whose close turns the fast line down after price penetrated below lower band, with no momentum divergence present.
- p.228 — Slow line = 16-period SMA of fast line; proxies intermediate momentum over 10–20 bars. Suspicious of trades against slow-line slope when it was extended and has turned toward zero. Slow line far from zero = trend confirmation. Structural tension: slow line extended above zero + fast line below zero → look for longs (inverse too).

## Multiple time frame regime interactions (Ch.7)
- p.230-231 — Dominant time frame is not always the higher one; control passes between time frames.
- p.232-235 — HTF overextension beyond Keltner band + momentum divergence VETOES LTF with-trend signals; HTF extension usually follows exogenous shock → much higher failure probability for LTF continuation trades.
- p.236 — Checklist for high-probability pullback (single TF): good impulse preceding; not overextended; NOT on third-or-later trend leg; no momentum divergence; no climax; pullback on lower activity/volume than trend legs.
- p.239-243 — MTF regime laws: (1) HTF trending → with-trend LTF patterns reinforced, higher win rates, sharper moves; (2) contra-HTF-trend patterns tend to abort; LTF countertrend trends tend to end exactly at ideal HTF with-trend entry points; (3) "When the higher time frame is ranging, expect sharp trends on lower time frames" — some of the cleanest trends occur inside HTF consolidation; (4) LTF ranges are often HTF continuation patterns → directional-breakout bias; (5) breakouts at HTF tipping points are outsized.
- p.240 — Range regime character: price action within ranges "more random and subject to sudden, large shocks"; levels rough; cleanest range trade = spring/upthrust.

## Relative strength (p.243-246)
- Leaders lead the trend in BOTH directions; buy leaders on weakness via pullback rules; disqualify if pullback makes a significant new momentum low on MACD. RS measure: ROC anchored to significant weekly-index pivot, or average of multiple ROC look-backs. RS screen = first-stage imbalance detector.

## Ch.8 quantifiable items with regime relevance
- p.249 — Active stocks average daily range a little under 3% of price. Fixed-% stops invalid across markets; volatility-based stops (N × ADR or N × 1-day SD) preferred. Initial stop guideline: rarely less than 2 ATR from entry, sometimes more than 4 ATR; under nearest pivot low.
- p.251-252 — Profit-taking: exit 25–33% at 1× initial risk; down to one-third-to-half by 2×.
- p.253-255 — Parabolic SAR flips constantly in chop (whipsaw = chop detector by implication); trailing use in trends only. Chandelier stop = fixed ATR multiple from trend extreme.
- p.255 — Anti-pattern: stopping out after 3 consecutive down closes in stocks is bad — "the market is usually primed for a bounce at that point."
- p.262 — Time stops: signals have edge only over limited horizon; no movement quickly → edge gone.
- p.264-265 — Trend vs range stop asymmetry (regime-diagnostic): "Price action in trends is more directional and more predictable than price action in ranges" → tight stops workable in trends (parabolic runaway: stop under previous day's low); tight stops NOT workable in ranges — large random spikes outside the range, lower liquidity.
- p.266 — Gap tendencies: most equity gaps reverse (gap-fill); high/low of day tends to be set early; gap down that immediately presses lower / breaks opening range down → good chance of trending down all day.
- p.273-274 — Volatility spike indicator (his monitoring spec): current day's change vs prior close in standard deviations of past 20 trading days; flag > ±3 SD. Uses 20-day HV (or max of HV and IV). Intraday tool: close-as-%-of-day's-range decile graph, red ≤20, green ≥80.
- p.269, 258 — Correlations move toward 1.0 in stress; correlation SHIFTS are the danger. Risk caps: ≤2–3× single-trade risk in correlated equities, ≤1.5–2× in correlated futures groups.
- p.287 (Ch.9) — "Many trading systems experience strings of wins and losses while the market is locked in one regime (trending or trading range)" — trade outcomes show serial dependence; Kelly assumptions break.
- p.280 — Minimum stop distance: never inside one average bar's range of the traded time frame (noise floor).

## Engine-actionable synthesis (Grimes's implicit classifier)
1. Chop/no-trade: price oscillating both sides of intermediate MA (~20-period), no significant departure → stand aside (p.208-209).
2. Chop-forming: spike through both 2.25-ATR Keltner bands in opposite directions → triangle regime, avoid (p.217).
3. Trend: price one side of MA, sloped MA, MACD fast line pegged, failed divergences accumulating, controlled proportional pullbacks on lower volume (p.207, 223, 226, 236).
4. Strong one-sided/climactic: bar-slide along a Keltner band or free bars (3–4% base rate) → imbalance extreme, no new entries, snapback risk (p.216, Table 7.1).
5. Transition: third+ trend leg, valid divergence after fast line touched zero, failure test at extreme, Anti setup, breakout-pullback failing via strong counter-momentum (p.199, 203-204, 227, 236).
6. HTF-range/LTF-trend: expect the sharpest tradable trends while the higher time frame ranges (p.239-241).
