Price / CVD Divergence

When cumulative delta refuses to confirm what price is doing — and how to read which way that resolves.

CVD (cumulative volume delta) = running total of aggressive buys minus aggressive sells (Σ AskVol − BidVol). It's the closest thing to a lie-detector on a move: price can print a new high on thin, unwilling participation, but CVD shows whether real aggressive volume actually pushed it there.

A divergence compares two price pivots of the same type — two highs, or two lows — and reads CVD at those exact same two timestamps. If price and CVD disagree about which pivot was "more extreme," you have a divergence. The whole art is in the two points being comparable and read at the same moments.

Read both series at the SAME two timestamps.
Regular → price makes the new extreme, CVD does NOT → reversal.
Hidden  → CVD makes the new extreme, price does NOT → continuation.

Who "leads"?

CVD leads, price lags. You never trade in the direction of the series that made the failed extreme — you trade in the direction CVD implies, and wait for price to catch up.

• Regular divergence: price over-extended (new high/low) with no CVD behind it → fade against price's failed extreme.
• Hidden divergence: CVD still pushing the trend while price only pulled back → go with the trend.

The four types

▼ Regular Bearish

price: HIGHER high  ·  CVD: LOWER high  → reversal DOWN

Price grinds to a new high but fewer aggressive buyers show up. Buying is being absorbed / exhausting.

→ Short the failed high (needs a bearish signal bar to trigger).

▲ Regular Bullish

price: LOWER low  ·  CVD: HIGHER low  → reversal UP

Price pokes a new low but sellers can't push CVD lower — buyers are absorbing the flush.

→ Long the failed low (needs a bullish signal bar to trigger).

▼ Hidden Bearish

price: LOWER high  ·  CVD: HIGHER high  → continuation DOWN

A pullback in a downtrend: price bounces to a lower high, but CVD spikes higher (short covering, not real buying).

→ Sell the pullback, trend continues down.

▲ Hidden Bullish

price: HIGHER low  ·  CVD: LOWER low  → continuation UP

A pullback in an uptrend: price holds a higher low while CVD flushes to a lower low (weak hands shaken out).

→ Buy the pullback, trend continues up.

Using it without cheating (causality)

A divergence is only tradeable if it was known at the moment you'd enter. That rules out the way I first built it (finding a "swing" that's only confirmed several bars later). The honest workflow — and the one you described — is:

  • Anchor on the current bar (the entry candidate). Its high/low is known now.
  • Compare back to a prior pivot that is already history (safely in the past).
  • Your example: three pushes up → a lower high with a bearish signal bar → look back to the prior high; if CVD there was higher, this lower high is a regular bearish divergence and the signal bar is your trigger.
  • The divergence is context, not the trigger — it needs a confirming signal bar (rejection / reversal bar) to act on. Divergence answers "which way is the tape lying?"; the signal bar answers "when."

⚑ CVD caveats. CVD resets each session, so only compare pivots within one session unless you carry it over deliberately. Aggressor classification depends on trade-side tagging (we use the quote embedded in each tick). And a divergence that never gets a signal bar never becomes a trade — most divergences "work" only in hindsight, which is exactly why the causal, signal-bar-gated version is the only one worth testing.
