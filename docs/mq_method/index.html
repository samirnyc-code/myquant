The MenthorQ method, extracted from source — and tested

Seven video transcripts from the MenthorQ Academy, transcribed and mined for every operational rule they state. Then every mechanically testable claim run against 1,159 sessions of 5-minute ES data (2021-06 → 2026-07) with our own ORATS-computed gamma levels and MenthorQ's published levels.

Two things this document separates carefully: what they teach (Part 1, quoted verbatim) and what survived testing (Part 2). They are not the same, and the gap is the interesting part.

The short version

TL;DR

  • Their levels are explicitly NOT support/resistance and NOT entry signals. They are take-profit targets, stop-placement anchors, and state classifiers. Every test that treats them as barriers is testing something they tell you not to do.
  • Their generic market craft validates well. The 1D expected-move band, the coin-flip open, the ATR breakout filter, volume confirmation — all measurable and real.
  • The gamma levels themselves showed no edge in eight independent framings. Including the two framings they actually advocate (expectancy over hit-rate; levels as targets not entries).
  • The method is not fully falsifiable as taught. It requires a discretionary "second confirmation" that they explicitly leave to the individual trader.
  • Major caveat: their traders in these transcripts are mostly trading blind spots, a separate product we have no data for. We may be testing the wrong tool.

Part 1 — as taught

The framework: Timing → Location → Setup

The organising structure of the whole method. Three questions, all must be yes.

1. TIMING — is this the right time to be in the market? 2. LOCATION — is this a place with good risk:reward AND protection? 3. SETUP — is my setup present, with a second confirmation? if any answer is NO -> no trade.

"ask the three questions on any freaking trade you take… if you get three yeses, take it. If you get only one single no, don't do it."

Setup

Timing

  • The first 30 minutes after 9:30 ET is "like flipping a coin" — high volatility, no directional information.
  • The 10:00 ET hourly close is said to give "much clearer direction".
  • Stand aside when price sits at the 50% equilibrium of a consolidation range with strong levels above and below — "supply and demand is equal".
  • Beware session overlaps (London/Europe close) — mechanical volatility.
  • Map volatility events first: economic calendar, earnings, expirations.
  • Timing can itself be the second confirmation.

Location

"Location means where we can place our trades with a good risk and reward… where the risk is first and where the reward is, and both have to make sense. If one makes sense and the other you're clueless — you shouldn't be entering."

Location

The distinctive idea here is protection — you want a level just beyond your stop, so the level defends the position:

"I have the one day max and call resistance protection… that's my team. If we go below this then I know my short is no more valid… if you have no strong areas where you can place your stop loss, then your stop loss is worthless."

Location

Target R:R quoted as 2:1 to 4:1. Look for extremes — highs and lows — not the middle.

Setup

"I see a strong rejection off a reaction zone… you get that hard wick rejection and then I'll wait for a confirmation candle. The next candle's formed, I jump in."

Setup — Dean

Setups are either a rejection at a reaction zone or a break of one. Always plus a personal second confirmation (order flow, wicks, VIX, moving averages — explicitly left to the individual).

2. Risk philosophy — hit rate is explicitly the wrong metric

"if you have a win rate from 90%, 80%, 70%… they will say to you you're doing the wrong job… cut your losses very fast. Sometimes you have only a win rate of 30%… you need only a few times but sometimes a very huge trade."

"the goal is really not to make you all the successful trade but to help you manage the risk. If you take away the bad trades, then suddenly the good ones become more relevant."

3. Wait for the reaction

"don't be adding shorts right away just because price comes up to a strong area. Wait for the reaction. Let price, let volume enter that level, then make a decision."

1. strong impulse move 2. technical correction (must stay below the 50% range — a lower high) 3. enter on the second impulse in the trend direction

Part 1 — as taught

Contradictions in their own material

Worth knowing, because they mean there is no single "house rule" to test.

CONFLICT

First touch vs second touch

Presenter A: "the first touch is the strongest one… with every next touch it becomes weaker and weaker."

Patrick: "he avoided trading the first test… waits for the second or third test."

UNFALSIFIABLE

The second confirmation

Required on every trade, but explicitly undefined: "Every trader is individual. Someone likes the order flow, someone likes the wicks, someone likes SMAs… there's no right, there's no wrong." A method with a free discretionary term cannot be mechanically falsified.

Two apparent findings were killed by our own error, not by the data. A "second touch rejects 97.6% of the time" result turned out to be survivorship (a 2nd touch only exists if the 1st didn't run away). A "+5.6pp level-chaining edge" turned out to be a control placed farther away than the real target. Both were caught before being reported as findings — but they are a warning about how easily this material generates false positives.

Part 2 — measured

What actually validated

The pattern across eight framings is consistent and worth stating plainly: their market craft holds up; their levels don't add measurable value over an arbitrary price at the same distance.

HOLDS

  • 1D expected-move band — 87.6 / 86.5 / 74.1 over 36 years, matching their published 87 / 85 / 73. Reproducible free from VIX.
  • The open is a high-volatility coin flip — 51.9% persistence, with the largest median move of the day (9.25 pts vs 6.75 later).
  • Clearing ±1 ATR predicts follow-through — roughly doubles median continuation (1.2 → 2.3 ATR). Sound trading logic.
  • Volume confirmation — points the right way (2.69 vs 1.99 ATR).

DIDN'T

  • Levels as barriers / repulsion
  • Levels as volatility predictors
  • Levels as move origins
  • Confluence / clustering
  • Level chaining as targets
  • Payoff-geometry advantage (expectancy)
  • The ATR rule being gamma-specific — it works at any price

Part 3

Gaps — what this does NOT settle

  • Blind spots. The traders in these transcripts mostly trade blind spots, not gamma levels — Dean's losing trade was at BL3, Steve's rejection at BL1. Blind spots are a separate MenthorQ product (overlap/correlation zones) and we have no data for them. It is entirely possible the wrong instrument was tested.
  • Intraday levels. Everything tested uses end-of-day levels — which MenthorQ themselves flag as the weaker version: "we're using end of the day levels… the condition during the day has changed". They are adding intraday futures levels.
  • The discretionary second confirmation. Required on every trade, deliberately undefined. Not falsifiable by construction.
  • Native futures-option levels. MenthorQ compute ES/NQ levels from CME futures options. ORATS carries none — so our ES/NQ levels are index-derived proxies and are not the same object.
  • Multiple comparisons. Eight-plus framings were tested. Any single marginal positive should be treated with suspicion on that basis alone.
